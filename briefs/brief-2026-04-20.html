The Beijing Countdown: Trump-Xi May 14–15 Summit Is the Most Important Credit Event Between Now and H2 Portfolio Reviews — With the Section 301 Hearing Landing the Same Day as the FOMC

On May 14–15, President Trump meets President Xi in Beijing. USTR Jamieson Greer confirmed the purpose on April 7: Trump is seeking "stability" — the diplomatic code for halting further tariff escalation while leaving Section 301 and Section 232 investigations as negotiating leverage. The summit was originally scheduled for March but was postponed due to the US-Israel war on Iran. That delay matters: the pre-summit period (today through May 13) is 24 days in which tariff escalation risk is highest, credit uncertainty is at its peak, and the Fed is simultaneously navigating its own policy meeting on April 28–29. The credit market is not fully pricing the complexity of what happens in this 24-day window.

The April 28 convergence is the single most overlooked risk in Q2 credit management. The USTR scheduled the public hearing on its Section 301 investigation — initiated March 11, covering China and 15 other countries — for April 28, the same day the FOMC begins its meeting. This is not coincidental: the Section 301 hearing will generate news flow about US-China trade escalation risk during the exact 48-hour window when the Fed is crafting its rate statement and forward guidance. If testimony at the Section 301 hearing is hawkish on China trade policy, it increases the probability that the FOMC statement acknowledges tariff-driven inflation risk more explicitly — which the credit market will interpret as a signal that rate cuts are further away. The dual April 28 event risk has not been flagged in any major credit market commentary this week.

The tariff backdrop entering the summit is transitional in ways that create its own uncertainty. The Supreme Court ruled in early 2026 that tariffs imposed under IEEPA were unlawful, forcing the administration to replace them with Section 122, Section 301, and Section 232 authority — a replacement process that is still in progress. On April 13, Trump threatened an additional 50% tariff on China in response to reported Chinese arms plans for Iran. US Trade Representative Greer and Treasury Secretary Bessent have both signaled that the May 14–15 summit is the mechanism for preventing that escalation from executing. The summit is, in effect, the thing standing between the current tariff environment and a 195% effective tariff scenario.

Synthesis — Commercial Credit Lens The 24-day window to May 14 is a credit positioning window, not a passive monitoring period. Three specific actions matter. First: any credit with supply chain exposure to China should be documented at current tariff levels and stress-tested at +50% (the threatened escalation scenario). If the credit only works at current tariff levels, it is effectively a tariff-hold bet — make sure that's an explicit decision, not an implicit assumption. Second: the April 28 dual event (Section 301 hearing + FOMC day 1) creates a 48-hour window of maximum credit market volatility. HY spread widening of 15–30bps is plausible in that window even without any new policy action — purely from news flow. If you have any credit decisions pending around April 28–29, bring them forward or push them to May 1+. Third: model the summit outcome scenarios explicitly: (a) de-escalation → tariff reduction, Section 301 investigation suspended → credit positive for China-exposed borrowers; (b) stalemate → current tariff environment maintained → base case; (c) escalation → 50% additional tariff executed → credit negative, model as you modeled the April 2025 Liberation Day shock. The summit has a binary-adjacent payoff structure. Don't manage it with a single base-case assumption.

Morgan Stanley's 8% Warning: AI Disruption Is the Default Catalyst Private Credit Covenants Were Never Designed to Catch — $728B in Software Exposure Is the Next Stress Test

Morgan Stanley issued a formal warning in March 2026 that private credit direct lending default rates will climb to 8% between H2 2026 and H1 2027 — driven primarily by AI disruption of software companies. The specific mechanism: as AI systems master complex workflows that previously required licensed software, demand for those software products erodes. Revenue declines. EBITDA declines faster (because software companies have high fixed cost structures that don't scale down with revenue). EBITDA-based leverage covenants are breached. The borrowers that entered private credit at 5–7x EBITDA in 2021–2023 can no longer service their debt at the EBITDA levels those multiples implied. Morgan Stanley estimates software exposure in direct lending at approximately 26% of a $2.8 trillion market — roughly $728 billion in software-company loans sitting inside private credit portfolios. Current trailing 12-month default rate (Fitch) is 5.8%, already the highest since the pandemic. Deutsche Bank projects defaults reaching 4.8–5.5% before year end; Morgan Stanley is 250bps more pessimistic, projecting 8%.

The mechanism by which AI disruption translates to credit default is not gradual — it is abrupt. Software company revenue in direct lending portfolios is typically measured quarterly via covenant compliance certificates. When an AI competitor enters a market, enterprise software companies often lose 10–20% of their customer base within two to three contract renewal cycles (roughly 12–18 months). The private credit covenant, typically set at EBITDA coverage of 1.0x or 1.1x, was designed to catch operational deterioration — not the structural revenue erosion caused by a substitute technology arriving at near-zero marginal cost. By the time the covenant breach appears on the compliance certificate, the underlying credit quality has been deteriorating for 12–18 months. Blue Owl Capital, which manages one of the largest direct lending platforms, limited withdrawals in two funds in April 2026 as redemption requests exceeded available liquidity — the first significant gate activity at Blue Owl since the 2022 rate shock. Morgan Stanley's North Haven Fund (approximately $8 billion in assets) honored only 45% of redemption requests after investors sought to withdraw 11% of NAV. These are not isolated incidents; they reflect a structural liquidity mismatch in private credit that is being exposed by rising defaults.

Synthesis — Commercial Credit Lens The Morgan Stanley 8% projection is a forward-looking estimate, not a current fact — but the current 5.8% trailing rate is already the highest since the pandemic, and the trajectory is unambiguous. For commercial credit professionals, the private credit stress creates three specific exposure vectors. First: direct. If your institution has participated in private credit syndications, CLOs, or co-investment structures, audit the software company concentration in those books immediately. Software companies at 5–7x EBITDA leverage that were originated in 2021–2023 are the population Morgan Stanley is describing. Run the 8% default scenario on that cohort and model the expected loss. Second: indirect via borrower overlap. Private credit and bank commercial credit share significant borrower populations in the $5M–$50M loan range. When private credit funds force defaults or restructurings on shared borrowers, bank lenders often find their own positions adversely affected — PIK interest accrual, covenant amendment requests, and restructuring requests follow private credit distress with a 60–90 day lag. Third: indirect via capital markets. Private credit funds honoring redemption requests sell their most liquid, highest-quality assets first. That means private credit forced sellers are active buyers of bank-originated commercial credit paper at discounts — creating price competition for your own credit book's market valuation if you need to sell or refinance.

The Debanking Final Rule: FDIC and OCC Eliminate Reputation Risk from Bank Supervision — Effective June 9, 2026. What Changes for Crypto, Cannabis, Firearms, and Oil & Gas Credit Access

On April 7, 2026, the FDIC and OCC jointly issued a final rule prohibiting federal bank regulators from criticizing or taking adverse action against a financial institution based on "reputation risk" — effective June 9, 2026. The rule goes further than deregulatory language: it explicitly prohibits examiners from requiring, instructing, or encouraging banks to close an account or terminate a product based on a customer's political, social, cultural, or religious views, constitutionally protected speech, or lawful business activities perceived to present reputation risk. The rule was published in the Federal Register on April 10 and directly implements Executive Order 14331, "Guaranteeing Fair Banking for All Americans," issued under the Trump administration's debanking initiative. FDIC Chairman Travis Hill issued a statement describing reputation risk as a concept that "increases subjectivity in banking supervision without adding material value from a safety and soundness perspective." The CFPB is simultaneously scaling back: reports indicate the agency may reduce its supervisory examination program from approximately 600 annual examinations to roughly 70 — an 88% reduction that functionally eliminates federal consumer financial oversight at scale.

The reputation risk concept has been the regulatory mechanism behind what critics called "Operation Chokepoint" and what the current administration calls discriminatory debanking — the informal pressure on banks to exit relationships with industries perceived as reputationally risky regardless of their financial performance or legal status. The industries most affected by reputation-risk-based debanking have been: cryptocurrency and digital asset companies; cannabis businesses operating under state licenses; firearms manufacturers, dealers, and distributors; oil and gas producers and pipeline operators; payday lenders; and certain defense contractors. With the final rule effective June 9, banks that previously cited reputation risk as the basis for declining these relationships or maintaining higher lending standards lose the regulatory cover for that position. The practical question for commercial credit: does the removal of reputation risk supervision lead banks to actively pursue these sectors, or does it simply remove a regulatory constraint while leaving residual board-level risk appetite constraints intact?

Synthesis — Commercial Credit Lens The June 9 effective date matters for commercial credit origination in three ways. First: sector reentry. Banks that previously exited cannabis or cryptocurrency sectors citing reputation risk concerns will face board-level questions about whether to reopen those books. The regulatory constraint is removed; the internal risk appetite and compliance infrastructure constraints remain. Expect a 60–90 day evaluation period at most major banks post-June 9, followed by selective reentry into the most creditworthy segments of those sectors. For commercial credit professionals, this creates an origination opportunity in cannabis multi-state operators (MSOs) with proven revenue and clean regulatory records — these were precisely the highest-quality credits that were most effectively shut out by reputation-risk supervision. Second: competitive dynamics. Banks that move first to re-establish credit relationships in previously debanked sectors will face less competition (few established relationships, pricing power) but more due diligence burden (limited credit history, novel risk factors). The first-mover advantage is real for sophisticated commercial lenders. Third: CFPB scaling back from 600 to 70 examinations changes the consumer credit compliance calculus materially. Banks with large consumer lending books now operate in a near-zero federal examination environment for consumer compliance — which shifts risk from regulatory enforcement risk to reputational and litigation risk. For commercial credit, the indirect effect is capital re-allocation: compliance resources freed from consumer regulatory preparation can be redeployed to commercial credit growth.

A borrower running $5.608 per gallon diesel against a surcharge rate calibrated for $4.80 diesel is absorbing approximately $0.83 per gallon unrecovered fuel cost. At typical fleet consumption levels, that is a material drag on operating cash flow that will appear in Q2 financials — three to four months from now.

California Diesel at Mid-$7 Range: The West Coast Premium Is Structural, Not Cyclical — And Fleet Card Portfolios Concentrated There Are Underestimating the Credit Risk

While national average diesel sits at $5.608, California diesel is running in the mid-$7 range — a $1.40+ premium per gallon above the national average. This premium is not a temporary Iran-war artifact; it is structural, driven by California's unique regulatory environment: CARB-mandated fuel reformulation requirements, the California cap-and-trade carbon pricing system, and limited pipeline connectivity that keeps California reliant on West Coast refinery output and Pacific Rim imports. The California diesel premium has been persistent and widening since 2022, and there is no regulatory or infrastructure change in the 2026 pipeline that would meaningfully compress it. Fleet card lenders with California-concentrated portfolios are underwriting at a cost of operations that is 25–30% higher than the national average — a difference that compresses carrier margins to the point where a single bad quarter can flip a borrower from covenant compliance to covenant breach.

The fleet card credit risk in California is compounded by California's aggressive enforcement of AB 5, which has effectively reclassified many owner-operators as employees, reducing the independent contractor base and concentrating trucking capacity in fewer, larger carriers. Those larger carriers have more negotiating power on freight contracts and can often negotiate fuel surcharges — but their leverage ratios are also higher, and their credit profiles are more sensitive to rate cycle turns. The sweet spot of California fleet credit — mid-sized carriers (10–50 trucks) that are too large to be owner-operators but too small to have enterprise-grade fuel hedging programs — is the highest-risk segment in the current environment.

Synthesis — Fleet Credit Action For any fleet card portfolio with California exposure exceeding 15% of book, run a specific sub-portfolio analysis using California diesel prices (not national averages). A carrier that looks borderline at $5.61/gallon national average looks significantly weaker at $7.00+ California diesel. Given the persistent California premium, any borrower running fixed-rate freight contracts in California is absorbing a structural cost disadvantage that cannot be hedged away operationally.

Credit Risk Macro

The Amend-and-Pretend Problem: Private Credit Is Using "Workout" Tools to Delay Software Defaults That Aren't Going Away — And the $728B Question Is How Long That Can Last

CNBC's March 25 report on private credit framed the industry's approach to rising defaults as the end of the "zero-loss fantasy" — the implicit narrative that private credit's EBITDA-based, floating-rate, covenant-lite structures would somehow outperform public credit through the cycle. The data now contradicts that narrative: 5.8% trailing defaults (Fitch), 8% projected by Morgan Stanley, and $9.5 billion in new CLO issuance in Q1 2026 alone — the latter not because the underlying credit is strong, but because private credit firms are issuing CLOs to raise cash as redemption requests outpace fund liquidity. The structural issue behind the data: private credit funds are deploying "amend-and-pretend" strategies — extending maturities, waiving covenant breaches, allowing PIK (payment-in-kind) interest accrual — to avoid recognizing defaults that are economically real but contractually deferrals. These are the same tools that European banks used between 2009 and 2014 to manage their post-financial-crisis loan books. The academic literature on that experience (Acharya et al., 2019) documents the cost: capital trapped in zombie borrowers cannot be redeployed to productive growth companies, credit supply to healthy firms contracts, and the deferred losses ultimately crystallize larger and more damaging than if they had been recognized contemporaneously.

The private credit amend-and-pretend dynamic is particularly dangerous in software company exposures because the underlying credit deterioration is driven by structural revenue erosion — not cyclical demand weakness. A cyclically weak borrower can be restructured to survive a temporary downturn: extend the maturity, reduce near-term cash interest via PIK, wait for the cycle to turn. A structurally disrupted software borrower facing AI substitution of its core product is not a cyclical problem with a time horizon fix. PIK interest on a company whose AI-driven revenue decline is permanent simply accumulates until the credit collapses at a higher face value. The Bloomberg April 2 CLO issuance story is the leading indicator of this dynamic: private credit firms are securitizing their existing books (including stressed software credits) to generate fresh capital, rather than recognizing the default losses on the books they've already deployed. The CLO market is, in effect, becoming the exit mechanism for private credit managers seeking liquidity before the full default cycle manifests.

Synthesis — Commercial Credit Action If you are receiving solicitations to participate in private credit CLO tranches or co-investment structures in Q2 2026, conduct specific due diligence on the software company exposure in the reference portfolio. Request: (1) the percentage of the portfolio in software/SaaS companies by revenue; (2) the weighted average vintage of those exposures (2021–2023 vintages are the highest risk); (3) the number of borrowers that have had covenant amendments, maturity extensions, or PIK elections in the trailing 12 months. A CLO with more than 20% software exposure, originated primarily in 2021–2023, with elevated amendment activity is carrying amend-and-pretend risk that the CLO structure's subordination may not fully absorb at Morgan Stanley's 8% default scenario.

CFPB Examining 70 Instead of 600: The Hidden Credit Risk in the Federal Consumer Oversight Vacuum

Reports indicate the CFPB will dramatically scale back its supervisory examination program — from approximately 600 annual examinations to roughly 70, an 88% reduction. For commercial credit professionals, this may appear irrelevant. It is not. The CFPB's examination function has served as the early warning system for consumer credit stress in small-dollar lending, auto finance, and student lending — all sectors that serve populations overlapping with commercial credit SMB customers. When the CFPB detects deceptive lending or systematic consumer distress in auto lending (as it did in 2022–2023), commercial credit portfolios with auto dealer exposure or fleet leasing exposure see early signals of credit stress through CFPB actions 12–18 months before it appears in default statistics. With 70 examinations instead of 600, that early warning system is effectively disabled. Commercial credit portfolios should increase their own monitoring of consumer-linked credit stress signals — delinquency data from FRED, charge-off rates at major consumer lenders, and NFIB survey responses on credit availability — to compensate for the reduced federal visibility.

Watch List Monitor Q1 2026 consumer credit charge-off disclosures from major card issuers (reporting in April–May). Rising charge-off rates in consumer credit lead commercial credit stress by approximately two quarters — particularly in SMB and owner-operated business populations where personal credit and business credit are closely linked. Any consumer charge-off rate above 4.5% at major issuers should be treated as a leading indicator for commercial credit portfolio stress in H2 2026.

The Debanking Rule and Small Business: Cannabis MSOs, Firearms Retailers, and Crypto-Native Businesses Are the First Movers to Watch for Bank Credit Reentry

The FDIC/OCC debanking final rule (effective June 9) creates an immediate SMB credit opportunity that most commercial lenders have not yet evaluated: the population of legally operating businesses that were previously denied or restricted from bank credit relationships on reputation-risk grounds. The clearest target market: cannabis multi-state operators (MSOs) with 3+ years of operating history, state regulatory compliance records, and audited financials. These businesses have been operating almost entirely on cash or through specialized non-bank providers (Safe Harbor Financial, Hypur, cannabis-focused credit unions) at substantially above-market rates. A bank that establishes credit relationships with compliant MSOs in June 2026 will have both a pricing advantage (MSOs have been paying 8–12% borrowing costs through non-bank channels) and a data advantage (3+ years of banking-alternative cash flow data that provides underwriting history even without formal bank statements).

The practical SMB credit risk framework for evaluating newly accessible sectors post-June 9: (1) Regulatory compliance record — a cannabis MSO or firearms dealer with a clean state licensing history has demonstrated operational discipline that is a valid proxy for credit quality; (2) Revenue diversification — a cannabis MSO with operations in 3+ states is less exposed to single-state regulatory reversal than a single-state operator; (3) Cash management history — the non-bank cash management records these businesses maintained during the debanking period provide underwriting-relevant financial history that can substitute for traditional bank statement analysis. The credit risk to price in: federal illegality for cannabis creates collateral limitations (no federally insured institution can hold cannabis inventory as collateral), and a future administration reversal of the debanking rule would create relationship instability. Structure credit accordingly: shorter maturities (24–36 months maximum), asset-based where possible, with covenant triggers tied to state licensing status.

Whatever It Takes: The Real Effects of Unconventional Monetary Policy
Viral V. Acharya, Tim Eisert, Christian Eufinger & Christian Hirsch · Journal of Finance, Vol. 74, No. 3 (June 2019), pp. 1063–1113

What the Paper Says

Acharya, Eisert, Eufinger, and Hirsch study the real economic effects of Mario Draghi's July 2012 "whatever it takes" announcement, which signaled that the European Central Bank would purchase sovereign debt of distressed eurozone countries without limit to prevent sovereign default. The announcement was widely credited with saving the eurozone — sovereign yields fell dramatically across periphery countries (Italy, Spain, Portugal), and the immediate financial market response was positive. Acharya et al. ask a harder question: what happened next, at the bank and corporate lending level?

Their finding is striking and deeply uncomfortable for monetary policy optimists. Banks that were beneficiaries of the sovereign debt support — specifically, banks with large holdings of periphery sovereign debt that rose in value after Draghi's announcement — used the balance sheet relief not to extend new credit to productive firms, but to "evergreen" loans to financially distressed, "zombie" borrowers that should have been allowed to default. The mechanism: the balance sheet relief gave banks the capacity to avoid recognizing losses on their worst credits, so they extended, amended, and restructured those credits to defer recognition. This evergreening behavior crowded out credit to healthy, productive firms — because the capital tied up in zombie loans was capital not available for new origination. The paper documents that non-zombie firms in regions with high zombie firm concentrations experienced significantly lower credit growth and employment growth than comparable firms in lower-zombie regions — a direct causal link from bank evergreening to real economic damage.

The quantitative finding: the ECB intervention reduced bank lending to healthy firms by approximately 5 percentage points in affected regions. The zombie firms that received the evergreened credit did not recover; they defaulted at higher levels than if they had been allowed to fail contemporaneously. The deferred losses were larger, not smaller, because zombie firms continued to consume working capital, management attention, and supply chain relationships that would have been redistributed to healthier competitors if allowed to fail. The paper provides the academic foundation for the "zombie firm" concept in credit markets and is the most rigorous empirical study of evergreening behavior at the bank portfolio level.

Why This Matters Right Now

Private credit funds managing $2.8 trillion in direct lending assets are not banks, and Draghi's 2012 intervention is not the same as the 2026 private credit stress. But the behavioral pattern Acharya et al. document is identical to what is happening in private credit today. Morgan Stanley projects 8% default rates in direct lending by H2 2026. The current trailing rate is 5.8%. The 220 basis point gap between those numbers represents the credits that are currently being managed through amend-and-pretend — maturity extensions, covenant waivers, PIK interest elections — rather than formal default recognition.

CNBC's March 25 framing — "the zero-loss fantasy is coming to an end" — identified the narrative. Acharya et al. identify the mechanism: private credit funds with stressed software company borrowers are extending those credits rather than recognizing defaults, because recognizing defaults would require marking down fund NAV, which would accelerate redemption requests, which would force further asset sales, which would compound the NAV decline. The evergreening is economically rational for each individual fund — it defers the pain. It is collectively destructive because it: (a) traps capital in economically dead software companies facing permanent AI disruption; (b) prevents that capital from being redeployed to the healthiest borrowers in the market; (c) ensures that when defaults do materialize, they come in a larger, more synchronous wave rather than being distributed over the default cycle.

The CNBC reference to "shadow defaults" — unreported defaults disguised by amend-and-pretend — is the private credit expression of what Acharya et al. called evergreening. The Blue Owl fund withdrawal limitation and Morgan Stanley North Haven's 45% redemption honor rate are the first symptoms of the mechanism reversing: when redemption pressure exceeds the fund's capacity to evergreen without asset sales, the mask comes off and the real default rate becomes visible.

Original Commercial Credit Translation — Not in the Paper

The Zombie Software Borrower Test: How to Identify Amend-and-Pretend Exposure in Your Portfolio Before It Forces Your Hand

Acharya et al.'s paper was written about banks and sovereign-debt-related evergreening. But the zombie borrower test they construct is directly applicable to private credit software exposures — and, for commercial lenders, to any borrower that has received covenant amendments or maturity extensions in the trailing 24 months. The key insight: zombie borrowers are defined not by their current default status but by their inability to service their debt at market rates. A software company that has received a covenant waiver because its EBITDA declined 25% due to an AI competitor losing customers is a zombie borrower — not because it has technically defaulted, but because its debt cannot be fully serviced at the original terms and the fundamental revenue trend has not reversed.

The commercial credit zombie test has three inputs, all of which should be available from your credit file: (1) Is the borrower currently unable to meet the original covenant levels, requiring amendment or waiver? (2) Is the borrower's revenue trend declining rather than recovering — i.e., is each quarter's performance worse than the prior quarter? (3) Is the reason for the decline structural (competitor, technology shift, secular demand decline) rather than cyclical (temporary demand weakness, one-time event)? If the answer to all three is yes, you have a zombie borrower — one that appears in your portfolio as "performing" (because the covenant was amended and payments are current via PIK) but is economically impaired in a way that amendment and extension cannot fix.

The critical policy implication from Acharya et al.: the sooner zombie borrowers are formally recognized and restructured or wound down, the smaller the ultimate loss and the faster the capital can be redeployed. This runs counter to every short-term incentive a credit officer faces — recognizing a loss crystallizes it, triggers ALLL provisioning, and surfaces a problem credit for management review. But Acharya et al. demonstrate empirically that deferral makes the problem worse, not better. The zombie software borrower that receives its third covenant amendment in 2026 while AI displaces 30% of its customer base is not "being worked out" — it is accumulating losses that will crystallize at a higher level in 2027.

The governance implication: establish a formal "zombie flag" in your credit review process. Any borrower that has received two or more covenant amendments in 24 months, with declining (not recovering) revenue trend, and a structural (not cyclical) cause, should be classified under a separate review framework that explicitly models exit or wind-down scenarios rather than renewal scenarios. The question to ask is not "can this borrower survive to the next maturity extension?" but "can this borrower's debt ever be repaid in full, and if not, what is the optimal resolution path?"

Implementation Path

  • Run a "zombie flag" screen on your current portfolio: identify any borrower that has received 2+ covenant amendments in 24 months, where revenue is declining quarter-over-quarter, and the cause is structural rather than cyclical. This population is your amend-and-pretend exposure
  • For each zombie-flagged credit, model the explicit wind-down scenario alongside the current renewal scenario: compare expected loss under each path, discounted for time value. Acharya et al. show that the evergreening scenario almost always produces higher ultimate losses — use their empirical framework to make the case internally for earlier resolution
  • Apply the zombie test specifically to any software company credit where AI disruption is a plausible revenue driver: request the borrower's customer attrition data for the trailing 12 months and compare against their underwriting-period customer retention assumptions. A 10% annual customer attrition rate that was 3% at origination is a structural red flag, not a cyclical blip
  • For private credit CLO participations and co-investments: request the amendment frequency report for the reference portfolio. High amendment frequency (more than 15% of portfolio receiving amendments in trailing 12 months) combined with software sector concentration is a composite zombie exposure indicator that Acharya et al.'s framework identifies as the highest-risk configuration
  • Reference Acharya et al. in your credit committee governance documentation as the academic basis for why "extend and pretend" is a loss-amplification strategy rather than a loss-management strategy — particularly valuable when the discussion is about granting a third covenant amendment to a deteriorating credit

The commercial credit translation (applying the zombie firm framework to private credit software borrowers under amend-and-pretend) is original synthesis with high face validity given Morgan Stanley's 8% projection and documented redemption gate activity in Q1 2026.

Steel-Manned Counterargument

The Acharya et al. zombie evergreening finding is specific to a context that differs materially from the 2026 private credit situation. The ECB intervention benefited European banks that were regulatory constrained — minimum capital ratios, sovereign debt haircut rules — in ways that created specific incentives to evergreen rather than recognize losses. Private credit funds are not bank-regulated, do not have the same capital ratio constraints, and operate under GAAP mark-to-model accounting that actually gives them more flexibility to recognize impairments gradually. The claim that private credit funds are evergreening in a way that is systematically destructive to capital allocation assumes that they lack the economic incentive to recognize and resolve bad credits. In reality, private credit fund managers face significant reputational and LPA-level consequences from sustained underperformance — LP advisory committees, co-investors, and successor fundraising rounds all create pressure to resolve rather than evergreen. The blue chip private credit managers (Apollo, Ares, Blue Owl, Golub, HPS) have sophisticated portfolio management operations specifically designed to identify and resolve zombie credits before they become capital allocation problems. The academic finding from a European sovereign debt crisis context may not translate directly to a US private credit AI disruption context where the market participants, regulatory framework, and incentive structures are meaningfully different.

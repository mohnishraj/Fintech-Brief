Liberation Day, One Year Later: The Tariff Shock Rewired How Commercial Banks Think About C&I Risk

April 2, 2025's "Liberation Day" tariff announcement triggered an immediate −5.7% single-day sell-off in the S&P 500 — the worst since 2022. One year on, markets recovered strongly (+16% from the nadir), but the commercial credit infrastructure tells a more complicated story. A Federal Reserve FEDS Note published January 2026 found that C&I loan utilization and spreads rose more significantly at banks with elevated exposure to tariff-sensitive supply chain borrowers. Crucially, this was demand-pull borrowing — companies drawing revolvers to pre-fund inventory and imported equipment ahead of tariff implementation — not distress-driven.

The slow-burn risk sits in equipment finance. ACT Research projects Class 8 truck prices rising approximately $10,000 per unit in 2026 from tariff pass-through alone. Fleet lenders who underwrote those loans at pre-tariff appraisal values are now sitting on a collateral gap that won't surface in charge-off data until Q3–Q4 2026. Meanwhile the Supreme Court struck down the broadest tariff provisions, but sector-level uncertainty persists — and the freight market's spot-contract convergence (see Macro Signal) is telling a story about carrier margin compression that the credit models aren't yet pricing.

Synthesis — Cross-Source Insight

The intersection of the Fed FEDS Note (demand-driven utilization spike) and ACT Research fleet price projections creates a specific, dateable risk: lenders holding transportation equipment as collateral are exposed to a classic appraisal-gap problem with a 12–18 month lag before it shows in charge-off data. Flag this in Q2 2026 portfolio reviews.

Bear Case → Most tariff-exposed borrowers drew revolvers as a precaution, not from distress — and many have since repaid. Tariff-driven reshoring is generating new domestic lending demand that partially offsets freight sector headwinds. The Supreme Court ruling reduces tail risk materially.

Trump Backs the Credit Card Competition Act — And Fuel Card Economics Are the Underreported Casualty

The Credit Card Competition Act of 2026 (S.3623), reintroduced by Senators Durbin and Marshall with bipartisan House support, cleared a major momentum threshold when President Trump endorsed it on social media, calling swipe fees an "out of control ripoff." The legislation would require large issuers — those with over $100 billion in assets — to enable routing competition, allowing merchants to choose from at least two unaffiliated payment networks per transaction. Current coverage focuses almost entirely on consumer credit cards and airline miles. The commercial card angle is being missed.

The $100B asset threshold captures many of the largest commercial card issuers in the U.S. The closed-loop properties that make fuel cards particularly valuable — real-time purchase controls, odometer verification, driver ID, site restriction — become technically complicated if mandatory open-loop routing applies. More concretely: fuel card rebates to fleets are partially funded by interchange revenue. If CCCA routing competition drives commercial card interchange toward the lower end of the spread (as the Durbin Amendment did for debit), the rebate pool that funds fleet fuel discounts shrinks. The challenger networks like Relay Payments — already priced below incumbent interchange economics — gain relative attractiveness.

Synthesis — Cross-Source Insight

This story isn't in the fleet card trade press yet. Model a 15–20% reduction in commercial card interchange under routing competition and quantify the pass-through effect on rebate-funded acquisition economics. The window to do this proactively is approximately 12 months.

Bear Case → CCCA implementation requires a multi-year rulemaking process. The banking lobby has blocked this bill in every prior session. Trump's endorsement may reflect political signaling rather than legislative priority. The bill has never passed a committee markup.

Private Credit's "Zero-Loss Fantasy" Is Cracking — And Banks Hold $1.14T in Exposure

Morgan Stanley warned in late March 2026 that default rates in private credit direct lending could surge to 8%, compared to the 2–2.5% historical average, with concentration risk in AI-disrupted sectors like SaaS middle-market. The $1.14 trillion that banks have extended to non-deposit financial institutions — private credit funds and BDCs — creates a direct transmission mechanism from private credit stress to regulated bank balance sheets. Banks' share of buyout financing above $1B collapsed from 61% in 2021 to 39% in 2023 as private credit flooded in; it has partially recovered to ~50% in 2025 as portfolio cracks appear.

Synthesis — Cross-Source Insight

Bank stress tests model direct C&I loss rates — not the indirect path through fund NAV deterioration and forced secondary market selling. The $1.14T bank-to-fund exposure is a non-linear risk that current stress scenarios likely underweight. Flag in Pillar 2 scenario analysis.

Bear Case → Morgan Stanley's 8% scenario is a tail risk, not a base case. Private credit BDCs have covenant packages that trigger renegotiation before technical default. Morgan Stanley itself said the scenario would be "significant but not systemic."

CFPB Cut from 1,700 to 550 Staff — Exams Down 88%: Compliance Arbitrage Is Back

The Trump administration's CFPB restructuring is materializing: headcount dropping from ~1,700 to ~550, supervisory exams from ~600 annually to approximately 70, all conducted virtually with narrowed scope. A court blocked full defunding — the agency is operational but dramatically reduced. The bureau received 6.6 million consumer complaints in 2025 per its April 2026 annual report, the highest on record, even as its capacity to act on them shrinks.

Synthesis — Cross-Source Insight

Non-bank SMB lenders face an 8–10 year supervisory holiday. History shows supervision vacuums in SMB credit fill with aggressively-priced products — MCA rate creep, revenue-based financing with undisclosed APRs. For bank commercial credit teams: adverse selection pressure from non-bank competitors operating without examination risk is the real story here.

Bear Case → A more focused CFPB may actually improve clarity for well-run programs. State AGs and the OCC are actively filling the gap. Reduced compliance uncertainty may expand credit availability for thin-file SMB borrowers.

The CCCA Is Not Just a Consumer Problem: Why Fuel Card Economics Are in the Crosshairs

The Credit Card Competition Act debate plays out almost entirely in consumer finance circles — airline miles, hotel points, Visa vs. Mastercard. What the coverage misses entirely is the commercial card stack, and specifically how the fuel/fleet card segment would be restructured if routing competition were mandated for commercial card rails.

The largest fuel card programs in the U.S. are issued by banks captured by the CCCA's $100B asset threshold: WEX's banking partnerships, Corpay's affiliated issuing relationships, and many white-label commercial card programs run through major bank networks. If these programs must route through an unaffiliated network, the closed-loop properties that make fuel cards valuable — real-time purchase controls, odometer verification, driver ID, site restriction — become technically complicated. Closed-loop networks derive value precisely from operating outside open-loop routing constraints.

The economics: fuel card rebates to fleets are partially funded by interchange revenue from the open-loop portion of transactions. Commercial card interchange rates average meaningfully higher than consumer rates for large-ticket categories — which includes diesel. If CCCA routing competition drives those rates toward the lower end (as Durbin did for debit interchange), the subsidy pool funding fleet rebates shrinks. Fleet operators face a choice between lower rebate rates or switching to non-regulated programs. Challenger networks like Relay Payments — already priced on lower interchange economics — gain relative attractiveness.

The timeline gives some runway. CCCA implementation requires a multi-year rulemaking process if passed. But the structural changes required — building or licensing competitive routing, renegotiating rebate economics with fleet operators, repricing merchant acceptance agreements — are not trivial. The window to do this analysis proactively is approximately 12 months before passage becomes a plausible planning scenario.

So What for Commercial Credit

If you underwrite fuel card programs or hold fuel card receivables: (1) model a 15–20% interchange reduction and quantify the effect on rebate-funded acquisition economics; (2) assess whether your program's closed-loop properties survive mandatory open-loop routing; (3) evaluate whether credit analyses of fuel card company borrowers have adequately priced interchange-dependency in their revenue models. Do this now, not after a markup is scheduled.

Thought Seed
🌱 Research Translation
An AI-Driven Credit Risk Assessment Framework for Strengthening SME Lending and Financial Stability
Syed Adil Abbas Rizvi · SSRN #5932478 · January 15, 2026 · ssrn.com/abstract=5932478
What It Says

A practical AI framework integrating ML models, alternative data, and explainable AI (XAI) for SME credit evaluation. The core argument: SMEs lack the financial reporting depth conventional underwriting requires, making credit invisibility a structural problem rather than a risk signal. XAI + behavioral data can reduce both false approvals and false rejections.

Why Not Yet in Commercial Credit

Most commercial credit teams use ML as a scoring overlay on traditional applications — not as a workflow replacement. The explainability requirement is hard. SR 11-7 model validation imposes material burdens on any ML model used in underwriting decisions. The paper largely ignores this, writing for an academic audience.

Original Commercial Credit Translation

The insight the paper doesn't make: the most commercially valuable application isn't SME loan origination — it's fuel card behavioral underwriting. Fuel and fleet card programs already generate continuous transactional data (spend frequency, category mix, geographic patterns, seasonal variation) that is richer than any application-stage alternative data. An XAI framework applied to this data enables dynamic credit limit management and early warning signals for carrier distress — without triggering SR 11-7 concerns, because the model outputs are monitoring signals, not origination credit decisions. That's a regulatory arbitrage that makes this paper commercially actionable right now.

Implementation Path
  • Map fuel card transaction data to the alternative data categories the framework identifies: spend frequency, category concentration, temporal patterns, network diversity
  • Build a behavioral distress score on historical accounts that subsequently defaulted — your portfolio already has the labels. XGBoost or LightGBM are strong starting points.
  • Design the output as a monitoring signal (not a credit decision) to stay outside SR 11-7 scope — the model recommends review, a human approves any limit change
  • Pilot on small-carrier accounts first (highest risk, richest data), then scale to mid-market fleet programs
  • Document explainability from day one: which behavioral features drive the score and why — essential for when a carrier disputes a limit reduction
Confidence Level
🟡 Medium — Technical approach is sound. The regulatory arbitrage framing is practitioner inference not in the paper. Pilot results in a specific portfolio could shift confidence significantly.
Steel-Manned Counterargument

The "monitoring tool, not credit decision" framing is fragile. Regulators increasingly view behavioral monitoring models that consistently drive automated limit reductions as functionally equivalent to credit models. The "human in the loop" requirement is only as strong as actual process discipline. Additionally, small carriers often operate with multiple fuel programs simultaneously, diluting the behavioral signal in exactly the highest-risk segment.

Practitioner's Take

Every bank has been doing this informally for years — your credit ops team notices when a good fleet account suddenly starts swiping at three different truck stops a week instead of their usual two. What this paper gives you is a reason to systematize and document that judgment. The XAI requirement is actually useful here — not because regulators demand it for monitoring tools, but because when a carrier calls in to dispute a limit reduction, you want to show them specific behaviors that triggered the review. The carrier who understands why their limit tightened is the carrier who gives you the context you need to make the right call. That's relationship banking with a ML assist.

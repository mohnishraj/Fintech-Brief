🚨 Diesel Hits $5.64 This Week — A 51.7% Surge Since February Is Blowing Up Trucking Margins in Real Time

The U.S. average on-highway diesel price reached $5.643 per gallon for the week ending April 6, 2026, according to EIA's weekly retail price survey — the highest since June 2022 and a sharp escalation from $3.72 in February. The EIA's Short-Term Energy Outlook now forecasts diesel will peak above $5.80/gallon in April before averaging $4.80 for the full year, a forecast that was set before this week's print. The proximate driver is a combination of tariff-driven crude cost increases, refinery seasonal transitions, and supply chain disruption from cross-border trade friction. Whatever the precise attribution, the operational math is brutal: at $5.64/gal and a best-case 7 mpg, fuel cost per mile is now $0.806 — meaning fuel alone consumes 48.5% of the average van linehaul spot rate of $1.66/mile.

That arithmetic leaves approximately $0.854/mile for every other cost category combined: driver wages, insurance, equipment, maintenance, tolls, financing. Industry benchmarks put non-fuel operating costs at roughly $1.10–$1.20/mile for a well-run small carrier. The gap is not theoretical — it is a live cash-flow crisis for the estimated 580,000+ small and mid-size carriers that depend on spot market rates. Fuel card utilization is almost certainly spiking as operators draw available credit to bridge a margin shortfall they cannot immediately pass through to shippers. The credit exposure is not just higher in dollar terms (51.7% more spend on the same gallon volume) — the underlying obligor stress has also increased dramatically.

⚡ SYNTHESIS — What This Means for Commercial Credit

Fuel card lenders are facing a compound exposure event that doesn't yet show up in charge-off data. When diesel spikes 51.7% in six weeks, utilization rises mechanically (the same tank costs more), but the obligor's ability to repay has simultaneously declined (margins compressed). Traditional credit models trained on 2020–2024 data have never seen this input combination — rising utilization driven by exogenous cost inflation rather than volume growth. The risk isn't just that carriers draw more credit; it's that the usual early-warning signal (rising utilization → monitor closely) is now firing simultaneously across thousands of accounts, masking which operators are in genuine distress versus simply experiencing a cost passthrough lag. Underwriters who can segment by freight lane (spot vs. contract), cargo type, and fuel network concentration will have a substantial information advantage in the next 60–90 days.

SBA Kills the FICO Score Shortcut for Small Business Loans — Effective March 1, Every 7(a) Lender Must Underwrite the Old-Fashioned Way

Effective March 1, 2026, the SBA formally discontinued use of the FICO Small Business Scoring Service (SBSS) Score for 7(a) Small Loans — the credit shortcut that allowed SBA-guaranteed lenders to approve loans up to $500,000 based heavily on a single composite score rather than full cash flow analysis. The change is codified in SBA Procedural Notice 5000-876777 (February 20, 2026) and represents one of the most significant underwriting methodology changes to the SBA program in over a decade. Under the new framework, lenders must demonstrate a Debt Service Coverage Ratio (DSCR) of at least 1.10:1 based on either historical or projected cash flows — and if the applicant cannot meet that threshold, the loan must be processed as a Standard 7(a) or SBA Express loan with full underwriting scrutiny.

The SBA's stated rationale is to align 7(a) Small Loan underwriting with "generally accepted industry credit analysis processes," and to allow lenders to use their own internal credit scoring models provided those models don't rely solely on consumer credit scores. In practice, this is a significant workload shift: the SBSS score was designed for speed and low analyst time. Replacing it with DSCR analysis increases processing time, requires more documentation from borrowers (two-plus years of tax returns, bank statements, business financials), and creates a de facto minimum liquidity threshold that the SBSS model did not enforce. For borrowers with strong cash flow but thin credit files — immigrants, first-generation business owners, gig-economy service businesses — this may actually improve access if DSCR is met. For thin-margin businesses with volatile revenue, it is a material tightening.

⚡ SYNTHESIS — What This Means for Commercial Credit

The SBSS sunset creates a structural gap in the SBA loan pipeline exactly when SMB demand is rebounding (NFIB Q1 2026: 22% of small business owners planning to borrow, up from 17% a year ago). The gap is not about credit quality — most businesses that qualified under SBSS would also qualify under DSCR. The gap is about processing time and origination capacity. Lenders that have invested in cash-flow-based underwriting automation (bank statement AI, real-time financial aggregation via open banking) are positioned to process the same volume with lower marginal cost. Lenders still relying on manual spreading will face a backlog. For fuel card and commercial payments portfolios, the secondary effect is interesting: if the 7(a) pipeline slows, small carriers and fleet operators who would have refinanced via SBA may turn to revolving commercial credit (including fuel cards and fleet cards) as a bridge. This shifts credit exposure from SBA-guaranteed to unguaranteed revolving — with direct P&L implications for fuel card lenders.

Federal Court Freezes CFPB Open Banking Rule — The Alternative Data Pipeline Fintechs Were Counting On Just Got Put on Ice

The CFPB's Personal Financial Data Rights Rule (Section 1033) — which required financial institutions with $250B+ in assets and non-depository firms with $10B+ in revenue to enable consumer-authorized data sharing by April 1, 2026 — has been effectively paused by a preliminary injunction issued by a federal district court in Kentucky. Judge Danny C. Reeves granted the injunction while the CFPB reconsiders the scope of the rule, blocking enforcement of the April compliance deadlines that were already in effect for the largest institutions. The rule was designed to operationalize open banking in the U.S., giving consumers the right to share their bank transaction data with third-party financial services apps — and giving those apps a legal, standardized pipeline to access financial data for underwriting and cash flow analysis.

The irony of the timing is sharp: the April 1, 2026 deadline was when open banking was supposed to move from theory to operational reality, enabling fintechs to use permissioned bank transaction data for alternative underwriting at scale. Instead, the injunction leaves the data aggregation ecosystem in legal uncertainty, while the court challenge — brought by bank-aligned industry groups — works through the courts. Moody's flagged the injunction in early 2026 as a significant setback for fintech lenders that had built underwriting pipelines anticipating standardized data access. For commercial credit specifically, the impact is asymmetric: large banks with direct data relationships are unaffected, while challenger lenders that depended on permissioned bank APIs for cash flow underwriting must either rely on less reliable screen-scraping or pause product expansion.

⚡ SYNTHESIS — What This Means for Commercial Credit

The injunction creates a two-tier underwriting market with a wider moat than most people are tracking. Banks with existing direct data relationships (T&E card data, deposit account data, payroll data) can continue building AI underwriting on their own data estates. Fintechs that were counting on Section 1033 to level the data playing field are now competing with one hand tied. For fuel card and fleet card lenders specifically, this matters because the most promising alternative underwriting signals — daily transaction velocity, network concentration, multi-card behavioral patterns — are only accessible if carriers share bank account data. Without a standardized legal pipe for that data, the best behavioral underwriting tools remain theoretical. The workaround is real-time payment network data from card transactions themselves, which doesn't require 1033 compliance. This is actually the advantage embedded fuel card lenders hold over bank-challenger models: they already sit on the transaction stream.

Class 8 Truck Orders Hit 46,000 Units in February — But at $238,000 a Truck, the Real Question Is Who's Financing the Mini-Prebuy

February 2026 Class 8 truck orders reached over 46,000 units — one of the highest monthly totals in the current cycle, per ACT Research — driven by a tariff-related prebuy surge as fleets raced to secure trucks before the 25% tariff on foreign-content heavy vehicles fully embedded in OEM pricing. ACT Research estimates tariffs alone add approximately $10,000 per truck, and when combined with the 12% Federal Excise Tax applied to the tariff-inflated base price, total new truck cost is approaching $238,000 for a standard Class 8 tractor — up from a base of roughly $170,000. The ATA has separately estimated that in extreme scenarios, tariff and excise stacking could add $35,000 or more per vehicle. The prebuy itself is historically bullish but structurally tricky: fleets ordering at record volumes in February are taking on equipment debt at elevated prices, in a freight rate environment that remains flat ($1.66/mile van linehaul), with diesel now at $5.64/gallon.

The financing dimension is underappreciated. Large fleet operators accessing manufacturer-captive financing or bank credit facilities at established rates can manage this. But for owner-operators and small carriers — who make up the majority of the trucking industry by unit count — the combination of higher purchase price and elevated operating costs creates a debt service scenario that may not close on a per-mile basis. Equipment lenders writing paper on new trucks at $238,000 in April 2026 need to model their obligors against diesel at $5.64 and rates at $1.66/mile to stress-test coverage. That math, at face value, does not work for a solo owner-operator on spot freight.

⚡ SYNTHESIS — What This Means for Commercial Credit

The Class 8 prebuy surge and the diesel shock are compounding from opposite directions into the same credit exposure: the carrier's balance sheet. Carriers that bought equipment at $238,000 in February financed their purchase assuming normal operating costs. They are now running diesel at $5.64/gal — an input they couldn't have hedged at signing. Equipment loan coverage ratios that were adequate at $3.72/gal are now borderline or negative at $5.64/gal. Fuel card lenders to these same carriers face rising revolving balances from the fuel shock while the carrier's debt service capacity is simultaneously compressed. This is the classic compounding-stress event: one shock (tariffs → equipment cost) creates the vulnerability; a second shock (tariffs → diesel) provides the trigger. The 60–90 day lag before delinquencies appear in credit data means the visible credit deterioration hasn't started yet.

SMB Borrowing Intent at Two-Year High — But the Tariff Shock May Flip the Credit Easing Narrative Before It Takes Hold

The January 2026 Senior Loan Officer Opinion Survey (SLOOS), covering Q4 2025 lending conditions, showed the most significant easing of small business credit since Q1 2022. The net percentage of banks reporting tighter standards for commercial and industrial loans to small firms fell to +4% — down from +18% in Q2 2025 and the lowest reading in four years. Simultaneously, 41% of surveyed banks reported increased loan demand from small businesses in Q4 2025, the strongest demand print in two years. NFIB survey data covering Q1 2026 shows 22% of small business owners planned to borrow within the next three months, up from 17% a year earlier. The macro context was favorable: the Fed held rates at 4.33% (after 75bps of 2024 cuts), inflation was moderating, and credit conditions were steadily normalizing after the 2023 tightening cycle.

That context has changed materially in the past 48 hours. The diesel shock announced this week — prices at $5.64/gal as of April 6 — is an inflationary input shock of a type that typically causes banks to reassess small business credit risk. Carriers, logistics operators, distributors, landscapers, construction firms, and food service businesses all have material fuel cost exposure. If fuel prices remain elevated into Q2 and Q3, the Q1 2026 SLOOS optimism may prove short-lived — with the Q2 2026 survey (due July) potentially reversing the tightening trend just as SMB demand was peaking. The window between easing credit conditions and rising credit stress is narrow, and we appear to be right at its edge.

⚡ SYNTHESIS — What This Means for Commercial Credit

The most dangerous moment in a credit cycle is not the peak tightening or the peak easing — it's the inflection where conditions are loosening while unpriced risks are building. Q4 2025 SLOOS shows banks relaxing. Q1 2026 demand is surging. New loans are being written. And this week, a 51.7% diesel spike materialized in the same portfolio. The credit-setting question is: are banks aware that the SMBs applying for credit today have operating cost structures that were calibrated to $3.52–$3.72 diesel? If not, the vintage quality of Q1 2026 commercial credit originations may be systematically worse than SLOOS-implied, because the key input cost inflated dramatically between the loan decision and the first repayment date.

The $5.64 Diesel Shock and the 60-Day Credit Clock: Why Your Fuel Card Portfolio Risk Just Changed This Week

There is a structural lag in commercial credit data that most practitioners understand abstractly but rarely experience in real time. Credit models train on historical data. Underwriting decisions reflect conditions at the time of the credit decision. And delinquency outcomes don't materialize until 30–90 days after the triggering event, depending on payment terms. The diesel shock of April 2026 is an almost textbook example of this lag — and the clock is running.

When diesel was $3.52 in January 2026, a typical owner-operator on a 150,000-mile annual haul was spending approximately $30,171/year on fuel (at 7mpg, 21,428 gallons × $3.52/gal). That same operator at $5.64/gal — the April 6 EIA weekly price — is now spending $42,214/year. That's a $12,043 annual fuel cost increase that materialized in six weeks. Most commercial trucking payment terms are net-15 to net-30. That means the operator's first inflated fuel bill — likely charged to their fleet card — already hit this week. The first payment due on that elevated balance is in the next 15–30 days.

The compounding factor is equipment debt service. A carrier that ordered a new Class 8 truck in February 2026 — paying the tariff-inflated price approaching $238,000 — is now servicing debt on a vehicle whose fuel cost has increased 51.7% since they signed. Monthly loan payment on a $238,000 truck at a 6.5% rate over 84 months is approximately $3,760/month. At $3.52/gal diesel and $1.66/mile rate, running 10,000 miles per month generated enough revenue to service equipment debt, pay fuel, and cover other costs. At $5.64/gal, that same 10,000 miles generates the same $16,600 in revenue but fuel now costs $8,057 (vs. $5,029 at $3.52/gal) — a $3,028 shortfall just in fuel, before anything else changed. Add equipment debt service and the cash flow statement goes deeply negative for a large portion of this cohort.

What does this look like in credit data? First sign: fuel card 30-DPD delinquencies beginning to rise in May 2026 (reflecting April transactions). Second sign: increased fuel card utilization (which could be misread as portfolio growth — it is not). Third sign: cross-account stress — an obligor who begins missing fuel card payments is also likely experiencing pressure on their equipment loan, insurance premium (up 8.8% sequentially as of Q2 2025), and any SBA or business term debt. The fuel card is often the first revolving facility to show distress because it's the most frequent transaction and most directly tied to operating cost. For portfolio risk teams, May–June 2026 30-DPD data will be the diagnostic.

The mitigation options are limited but real. First: segment the portfolio by carrier size and freight lane immediately. Owner-operators on spot van are the highest-risk cohort. Long-haul contract carriers with fuel surcharge provisions in shipper contracts have partial pass-through protection. Tanker and hazmat carriers typically have higher rates and better margin cushion. Second: activate behavioral monitoring now — flag accounts with utilization spikes exceeding 30% week-over-week. Third: evaluate whether credit line increases in the next 60 days serve the customer (bridge to recovery) or extend an insolvent obligor's runway at portfolio expense. The distinction requires cash flow underwriting that most fuel card programs don't currently perform at the individual account level.

⚙️ SO WHAT FOR COMMERCIAL CREDIT

The diesel shock is a portfolio segmentation event, not a binary stress event. The owner-operator on spot van is in existential distress at $5.64/gal. The large fleet with contract rates and fuel surcharges is annoyed but functional. The medium-size regional carrier is somewhere in between and dependent on their specific lane mix and customer contractual terms. Any fuel card risk team that treats the portfolio as a single exposure to "trucking risk" in April 2026 is misreading the situation. The operational urgency: pull utilization spike alerts, segment by carrier type, and identify the 90th percentile utilization accounts before the May billing cycle closes. Those accounts should be on manual review before Q2 collections season begins.

This processing delay — likely 2–4 additional weeks per application during the transition — means small business applicants approved in March–April 2026 are waiting longer for SBA capital. Many of these applicants are in freight, logistics, and service industries with fuel cost exposure. During the processing gap, they are drawing on existing revolving facilities — including fuel cards — to bridge operating costs. This is creating an invisible demand surge in fuel card utilization that has nothing to do with driving volume. Risk: the SBA loan eventually funds and the fuel card balance clears — or the business deteriorates during the wait and the bridge credit becomes permanent.

Open Banking Injunction Creates a Data Asymmetry Window That Will Close Abruptly

The Kentucky court injunction on Section 1033 has paused open banking enforcement for the largest institutions — but bilateral data-sharing agreements between banks and aggregators (Plaid, MX, Finicity) that predate the rule are unaffected. Large fintechs with direct integration agreements continue to access permissioned bank data. Smaller fintechs and new entrants cannot access it at scale. This creates a temporary data asymmetry: established players are building ML underwriting on richer data than competitors. When the injunction is resolved — either by the court dissolving it or the CFPB revising the rule — the data gap closes rapidly. New entrants who haven't built bilateral agreements during this window will face a steeper ramp when compliance resumes. The asymmetry window is likely 6–18 months.

Thought Seed
🌱 THOUGHT SEED — RESEARCH TRANSLATION
AI and Machine Learning in Credit Risk Assessment: Exploring the Transformative Role in Modern Banking
Dr. Aakash Ramchand Dil, PhD (Maths), DBA (Risk Management), CQF | SSRN Working Paper No. 5260027 | Free Access

WHAT IT SAYS

The paper examines the transition from traditional credit scoring (FICO, rule-based models) to machine learning-driven credit risk assessment in modern banking. It covers supervised learning approaches (gradient boosting, random forests, neural networks) applied to default prediction, the integration of alternative data sources (transaction data, behavioral signals, social and operational data), and the regulatory implications of model explainability under fair lending requirements. The paper argues that ML models outperform traditional scorecards on predictive accuracy but create new challenges in model governance, bias detection, and regulatory compliance — particularly under evolving frameworks like the EU AI Act (effective August 2026 for credit scoring).

WHY NOT YET IN COMMERCIAL CREDIT

The paper's empirical examples are drawn almost entirely from consumer credit and retail banking datasets. Commercial credit — and specifically fuel card and fleet card credit — operates on fundamentally different data structures: transaction-level behavioral data from a narrow use case (fuel purchases), multi-card obligors (a fleet with 50 cards is one credit exposure with 50 behavioral signals), and credit quality that is more correlated to external commodity prices (diesel) than to borrower personal finance behavior. The paper's ML frameworks don't model this commodity-credit correlation at all. Additionally, commercial credit underwriting typically involves longer decision cycles, more negotiated terms, and less volume than consumer credit — which makes the ML "big data" advantage smaller in commercial than in consumer contexts.

⚡ ORIGINAL COMMERCIAL CREDIT TRANSLATION — Not In the Paper

The insight the paper doesn't make: in fuel card lending, the most powerful ML signal isn't the borrower's credit file — it's the behavioral signature of stress within the fuel transaction data itself. Specifically, carriers under cash flow pressure exhibit three detectable patterns before the first missed payment: (1) Network drift — they shift from preferred discount network stations to higher-cost convenience locations, indicating they are no longer routing to optimize fuel spend but simply stopping when the tank runs dry; (2) Cadence compression — fill-up intervals shorten from every 700–900 miles to every 400–500 miles as drivers reduce loads or change routes to reduce total fuel outlay; and (3) Time-of-day shift — fueling moves from daytime to 10pm–5am windows as drivers bypass weigh stations and reduce peak-rate exposure. These signals are invisible in credit bureau data, SBSS scores, or DSCR analysis. They are only visible in fuel card transaction streams. A carrier in genuine distress will show all three 45–75 days before the first delinquency. This is the behavioral underwriting play that ML on fuel card transaction data actually enables — and it has no analog in any paper on consumer ML credit risk.

IMPLEMENTATION PATH

  • Step 1: Build a labeled dataset of carrier accounts that went delinquent in prior stress periods (2020, 2022 diesel spike), tagged with fuel card transaction data 90 days prior
  • Step 2: Engineer network drift, cadence compression, and time-of-day shift features at the account level using weekly aggregation windows
  • Step 3: Train a gradient boosting classifier on these behavioral features alongside traditional credit features; validate that behavioral features improve AUC vs. credit-only model
  • Step 4: Deploy as an early-warning score refreshed weekly on all active accounts; set alert thresholds at top decile for manual underwriter review
  • Step 5: A/B test proactive outreach (credit line adjustment, rate modification offer) to early-warning accounts vs. control — measure delinquency reduction
🟢 Confidence Level: High The behavioral signal theory is grounded in established credit early-warning literature and domain knowledge of fuel card networks. The specific feature engineering (network drift, cadence) is novel but mechanistically sound.

STEEL-MANNED COUNTERARGUMENT

The behavioral signals described (network drift, cadence compression, time-of-day shift) may not be distress signals — they may be operational adaptations to tariff-driven route changes. In April 2026, tariffs have altered cross-border routes, nearshoring has shifted freight lanes, and capacity exits have changed available loads. A carrier shifting to overnight fueling may be running a new long-haul lane, not hiding from weigh stations. Network drift to higher-cost stations may reflect route changes to serve new customers, not cash flow desperation. Any ML model trained on 2020 or 2022 patterns would need to be carefully re-validated against 2026 operational context before deployment. Behavioral signals are correlated with distress, not causally diagnostic — and in an unusual macro environment, false positive rates could be very high.

PRACTITIONER'S TAKE

The paper gives you the ML toolkit. It doesn't give you the use case. In fuel card lending, the use case is this: you are sitting on the richest real-time behavioral dataset in all of commercial credit — transaction by transaction, gallon by gallon, location by location — and the only thing most programs do with it is fraud detection and spend controls. If you built an early-warning model using these features and deployed it before this week's diesel spike, you would already know which accounts in your portfolio are showing the stress signature. Instead, you're probably going to find out when the first May delinquencies hit. That 45-day gap is the opportunity cost of not having built the model yet.

Liberation Day, One Year Later: The Tariff Shock Rewired How Commercial Banks Think About C&I Risk

April 2, 2025's "Liberation Day" tariff announcement — sweeping country-specific duties beginning at 10% with higher rates for major trading partners — triggered an immediate -5.7% single-day sell-off in the S&P 500, the worst since 2022. One year on, the market recovered strongly (+16% from the Liberation Day nadir), but the commercial credit infrastructure tells a more complicated story. A Federal Reserve FEDS Note published January 2026 found that C&I loan utilization and spreads rose more significantly at banks with elevated exposure to tariff-sensitive supply chain borrowers — driven not by lenders pushing credit but by borrowers pulling revolvers to pre-fund inventory and imported equipment costs ahead of tariff implementation dates.

The borrowing surge appears to have been demand-pull, not supply-push — a critical distinction for risk officers. Where credit quality has since held, it reflects that most tariff-exposed borrowers drew lines as a precautionary measure, not a distress signal. However, ACT Research projects that Class 8 truck prices will rise approximately $10,000 per unit in 2026 from tariff pass-through alone — and fleet lenders pricing equipment at origination have almost certainly underwritten to pre-tariff collateral values. That's a slow-burn credit quality gap accumulating in transportation portfolios right now. Meanwhile, the Supreme Court struck down the original tariff regime's broadest provisions, but implementation remains contested and sector-level uncertainty persists.

Synthesis — What No Single Source Says The intersection of Fed FEDS Note data (demand-driven C&I utilization spike) and ACT Research fleet price projections creates a specific risk pattern: borrowers who drew revolvers in 2025 to front-load equipment purchases are now sitting on assets whose replacement cost exceeded appraisal by ~$10K/unit. If freight volumes normalize slower than expected — and the spot-contract rate convergence (see Macro Signal) suggests they might — lenders holding transportation equipment as collateral are exposed to a classic appraisal-gap problem with a 12-18 month lag before it shows in charge-off data. This is worth flagging in Q2 2026 portfolio reviews.

Trump Backs the Credit Card Competition Act: The Biggest Threat to Commercial Card Economics Since Dodd-Frank

The Credit Card Competition Act of 2026 (S.3623), reintroduced by Senators Durbin and Marshall with bipartisan House support, cleared a major momentum threshold when President Trump publicly endorsed it on social media, calling swipe fees an "out of control ripoff." The legislation would require large credit card issuers — those with over $100 billion in assets — to enable routing competition by offering merchants a choice of at least two unaffiliated payment networks per transaction. This directly targets Visa and Mastercard's near-exclusive routing arrangement dominance. An analysis from Payments Dive notes that this could structurally reprice interchange across the card industry, and legal analysts at KTS Law and Lexology note the bill raises novel questions about whether large commercial card programs (including fuel/fleet cards issued by banks above the threshold) would be captured under the asset-size provision.

The commercial credit angle is underappreciated in the current press coverage. Most analysis focuses on consumer credit cards. But fuel cards issued by banks like WEX (which has bank partnerships) and programs affiliated with issuers above the $100B threshold face a potentially different competitive landscape. If routing competition forces down interchange on commercial cards, the rebate-funded value proposition underpinning fleet card adoption — where fleets accept card discipline in exchange for fuel rebates funded partly by interchange — compresses. Fuel card economics are also built on the assumption of premium interchange relative to general purpose cards. That assumption is now in legislative play.

Synthesis — What No Single Source Says The CCCA's commercial card exposure is not being covered in fleet or commercial credit trade press yet. The focus on consumer cards is understandable — consumer interchange is the primary target — but the asset-size threshold ($100B+) captures many of the largest commercial card issuers in the U.S. If the bill passes and routing competition applies to commercial as well as consumer rails, the fuel card rebate model faces its most fundamental structural challenge since chip+PIN killed magstripe fraud economics. This is a scenario worth stress-testing in commercial card revenue projections.

Private Credit's "Zero-Loss Fantasy" Cracks: Default Rates Could Hit 8% — and Banks Are Circling

Morgan Stanley issued a stark warning in late March 2026: default rates in private credit direct lending portfolios could surge to 8%, compared to the historical average of 2–2.5%, with concentration risk in sectors vulnerable to AI disruption (particularly software-as-a-service middle-market companies). The $1.14 trillion that banks have extended to non-deposit financial institutions — largely private credit funds and BDCs — creates a transmission mechanism from private credit stress to regulated bank balance sheets. Banks' direct share of buyout financing above $1 billion collapsed from 61% to 39% between 2021 and 2023 as private credit flooded in; by 2025 it had partially recovered to ~50% as cracks appeared in private credit portfolios.

For commercial credit practitioners, the key question is whether private credit stress is a leading indicator for bank C&I credit quality. The historical answer has been yes — private credit portfolios often hold the same borrowers as bank syndicated facilities, just at lower lien priority and higher leverage. Moody's Chief Economist Mark Zandi noted in March 2026 that this is "an opportune time for banks to regain market share from private credit funds." The regaining of share isn't purely altruistic: banks with experienced commercial credit teams can now compete for middle-market deals at pricing that was previously dominated by private credit, while private credit funds carrying stressed portfolios may be forced to sell positions at discounts — creating secondary market opportunity for sophisticated bank credit buyers.

Synthesis — What No Single Source Says The private credit stress narrative is typically framed as a private fund problem. But the $1.14T in bank loans to non-deposit financial institutions creates a correlation risk: the same macro forces (tariff uncertainty, AI disruption of SaaS middle-market) that stress private credit portfolios also stress the banks' exposure to those funds. This is a double-counting problem that aggregate bank stress tests may not adequately capture, since the test scenarios typically model direct C&I loss rates — not the indirect path through fund NAV deterioration. For commercial credit teams at large banks, this is worth flagging in Pillar 2 scenario analysis.

CFPB Shrinks from 1,700 to 550 Staff — Examinations Cut 88%: Compliance Arbitrage Is Back

The Trump administration's plan to restructure the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau is materializing rapidly. Reported plans would cut headcount from approximately 1,700 authorized employees (pre-Trump) to roughly 550 — a 68% reduction — while supervisory examinations could drop from ~600 annually to approximately 70, an 88% decline. All examinations would reportedly be conducted virtually and with narrowed scope. A federal court ruled against the administration's attempt to withhold CFPB funding entirely, meaning the agency continues to operate — but at dramatically reduced enforcement and supervisory capacity. The bureau received 6.6 million consumer complaints in 2025, up from previous years, per its annual report published in April 2026.

The commercial credit implications extend beyond consumer-facing products. Fair lending examinations for commercial card programs — particularly those involving small business owners who are personally guaranteeing commercial credit — fall within CFPB's supervisory scope for institutions above certain asset thresholds. With examinations dropping 88%, the practical probability of a given commercial lender facing CFPB supervisory review in any given year drops near zero. This is a double-edged outcome: reduced compliance burden for well-run programs, but also reduced oversight of predatory practices in the commercial and SMB lending space — an area where the bureau had been expanding its attention in 2024-2025.

Synthesis — What No Single Source Says The 88% examination cut creates a compliance arbitrage environment that hasn't existed since before Dodd-Frank. Fintech lenders and non-bank commercial credit providers — who were disproportionately subject to CFPB supervisory expansion under the prior administration — now face an 8-10 year supervisory holiday. History suggests that reduced supervisory presence in credit markets tends to expand credit availability in the short run and worsen credit quality in the medium run. For commercial credit risk managers at banks, this means competitors in the non-bank lending space may now offer terms that would not have passed examination scrutiny two years ago — creating adverse selection pressure in the SMB and commercial credit markets.

Bank M&A Wave Accelerates: 181 Deals in 2025 — Commercial Credit Portfolios Are the Prize

U.S. bank M&A recorded 181 deals in 2025 — a 45% year-over-year increase — and analysts at Klaros Group predict 2026 could see double that figure, driven by pent-up consolidation pressure from slower activity earlier in the decade and a markedly faster regulatory review timeline under the current administration. The industry structure is shaking out into a "barbell": mid-tier and regional banks growing via acquisition, while sub-$1B institutions cluster on the other end. States with the highest bank concentration — Texas, Illinois, Ohio, Minnesota, Pennsylvania — are expected to see the most deal activity. Klaros also anticipates at least 25 de novo bank charter applications in 2026, the most in decades.

For commercial credit practitioners, consolidation creates immediate portfolio integration complexity. Acquired commercial credit portfolios often carry different underwriting standards, pricing models, and covenant structures — and the acquiree's risk appetite may not match the acquirer's. In a tariff-uncertain environment with potential private credit stress, integrating a commercial loan book from a bank with meaningful freight or manufacturing sector exposure requires a careful segmentation analysis. The flip side: consolidation creates larger institutions with greater appetite for commercial card program investment, larger fuel card issuing capacity, and better data infrastructure for AI-driven credit decisioning.

Synthesis — What No Single Source Says The 181-deal figure plus the de novo activity tells a bifurcated story: large institutions getting larger via acquisition, while new charters target niches the consolidating banks are vacating. For commercial credit and fuel card programs, the relevant question is which niches are being vacated. Evidence from prior consolidation waves suggests that small-fleet commercial card programs and SMB-focused commercial credit lines are often de-prioritized post-merger, creating market share opportunity for fintech and non-bank competitors — exactly at the moment CFPB supervision is diminishing.

The Credit Card Competition Act Is Not Just a Consumer Problem: Why Fleet Card Economics Are in the Crosshairs

The Credit Card Competition Act debate plays out almost entirely in consumer finance circles. The narrative centers on large banks, Visa, Mastercard, and the retailers who pay interchange — with occasional acknowledgment of airline miles and hotel points as potential casualties of a more competitive routing environment. What the coverage misses entirely is the commercial card stack, and specifically how the fuel/fleet card segment would be restructured if routing competition were mandated for commercial card rails.

The current commercial card interchange architecture is not identical to consumer interchange — but it is more intertwined than most assume. The largest fuel card programs in the U.S. are issued by banks that would be captured by the CCCA's $100 billion asset threshold: WEX's banking partnerships, Corpay's affiliated issuing relationships, and many white-label commercial card programs run through the major bank networks. If these programs are required to route through an unaffiliated network for at least one rail option, the closed-loop properties that make fuel cards particularly valuable — real-time purchase controls, odometer verification, driver ID, site restriction — become technically complicated. Closed-loop networks derive much of their value precisely from operating outside open-loop routing constraints.

The economics matter most. Fuel card rebates to fleets are partially funded by interchange revenue from the open-loop portion of fuel card transactions. Current commercial card interchange rates average meaningfully higher than consumer rates for large-ticket categories — which includes diesel purchases. If CCCA routing competition drives those rates toward the lower end of the spread (as the Durbin Amendment did for debit interchange), the subsidy pool that funds fleet fuel rebates shrinks. Fleet operators would face a choice between lower rebate rates or absorbing the cost of switching to non-regulated programs. The competitive advantage of the large incumbent fuel card networks narrows, and challenger networks like Relay Payments — which operate on lower interchange economics — gain relative attractiveness.

The timeline matters too. CCCA implementation, if passed, would trigger a multi-year rulemaking process. Fuel card programs have time to restructure — but not infinite time, and the structural changes required (building or licensing competitive routing, renegotiating rebate economics with fleet operators, repricing merchant acceptance agreements) are not trivial. The commercial credit risk angle is distinct: banks holding fuel card receivables need to think about whether their underwriting models correctly price the revenue-model risk embedded in those portfolios, not just the credit risk of the underlying fleet operators.

So What for Commercial Credit

If you underwrite fuel card programs or hold fuel card receivables, the CCCA introduces a structural revenue risk that belongs in your credit memos and model documentation — not just as a tail risk, but as a plausible base-case scenario given Trump's endorsement and bipartisan Senate sponsorship. Specifically: (1) model a 15-20% reduction in commercial card interchange under routing competition and quantify the pass-through effect on rebate-funded fuel card acquisition economics; (2) assess whether the closed-loop network properties of your program would survive mandatory open-loop routing, or whether network restructuring is required; (3) evaluate whether your credit analysis of fuel card company borrowers has adequately captured interchange-dependency in their revenue models. The window to do this analysis proactively — rather than reactively — is approximately the next 12 months.

Thought Seed
🌱 Research Translation
An AI-Driven Credit Risk Assessment Framework for Strengthening SME Lending and Financial Stability
Syed Adil Abbas Rizvi · SSRN Working Paper #5932478 · January 15, 2026 · ssrn.com/abstract=5932478

What It Says

This paper presents a practical AI-driven credit risk assessment framework integrating machine learning models, alternative data sources, and explainable AI (XAI) mechanisms to support transparent, data-driven credit evaluation for SME lending. The framework targets a known weakness in traditional credit scoring: that SMEs often lack the financial reporting depth (audited statements, 3-year P&L histories) that conventional underwriting requires, making credit invisibility a structural problem rather than a risk signal. The paper argues that behavioral and transactional alternative data — combined with XAI to maintain regulatory transparency — can reduce both Type I and Type II errors in SME credit decisions.

Why Not Yet in Commercial Credit

Most commercial credit underwriting teams that have adopted ML are using it as a scoring overlay on traditional applications — not as a replacement for the application-and-spreading workflow. The "explainability" problem is genuinely hard: commercial credit involves relationship management and covenant negotiation that require human judgment the paper doesn't address. Additionally, the largest commercial credit practitioners (the major banks) operate under Model Risk Management frameworks (SR 11-7) that impose material validation burdens on any ML model used in underwriting — burdens the paper largely ignores because it's written for the policy/academic audience, not practitioners.

Original Commercial Credit Translation

The insight the paper itself does not make: the most commercially valuable application of this framework is not in SME loan origination — where relationship banking and covenant documentation will remain dominant — but in commercial card behavioral underwriting. Fuel and fleet card programs already generate a continuous stream of transactional data (spend frequency, category mix, geographic patterns, seasonal variation) that is richer than any application-stage alternative data set. An XAI framework applied to fuel card spend behavior could enable dynamic credit limit management and early warning signals for carrier distress — without triggering Model Risk Management concerns because the model's outputs are behavioral monitoring tools, not origination credit decisions. This is a regulatory arbitrage that makes the research commercially actionable in a way that SME loan origination is not.

Implementation Path

  • Map your existing fuel card transaction data to the alternative data categories the framework identifies (spend frequency, category concentration, temporal patterns, network diversity)
  • Build a behavioral distress score using XGBoost or LightGBM on historical accounts that subsequently defaulted — your portfolio already has the labels
  • Design the output as a monitoring signal (not a credit decision) to stay outside SR 11-7 model validation requirements — the model recommends additional review, a human approves any limit changes
  • Pilot on a cohort of small-carrier fuel card accounts (the highest-risk, most data-rich segment) before scaling to mid-market fleet programs
  • Document explainability from day one: which behavioral features drive the score, and why they correlate with payment distress in your specific portfolio

Steel-Manned Counterargument

The regulatory arbitrage argument (monitoring tool vs. credit decision) is fragile. Regulators and examiners increasingly view behavioral monitoring models that inform limit changes as functionally equivalent to credit models — especially if the behavioral score consistently drives automated limit reductions without human review in practice. The "human in the loop" requirement is only as strong as the actual process discipline. Additionally, fuel card spend patterns may not be as predictive as the framework assumes for small carriers who operate with multiple fuel programs simultaneously, diluting the behavioral signal in exactly the highest-risk segment.

Practitioner's Take

Look, every bank has been doing something like this informally for years — your credit ops team notices when a good fleet account suddenly starts swiping at three different truck stops in a week instead of their usual two. What this paper gives you is a reason to systematize and document that judgment. The XAI requirement is actually useful here — not because regulators demand it for monitoring tools, but because when a carrier calls in to dispute a limit reduction, you want to show them specific behaviors that triggered the review, not a black box number. The carrier who understands why their limit tightened is the carrier who gives you the context you need to make the right call. That's not a model story — that's a relationship banking story with a ML assist.
